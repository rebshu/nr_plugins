01 — Core product domain

COGS & organizational intelligence

Mapping what a decision actually is — and making it legible
Strong fit
Why strong
Kelly decomposes every question before answering it. Rebecca builds architecture before features. Johny's elimination mode finds what the framework is missing. All three are wired to interrogate framing — which is exactly what the COGS thesis requires.
Risk
The product is the thinking, which means the team could over-engineer the framework before testing it with customers. Needs explicit forcing functions to ship v1.
Who leads
Kelly on question decomposition architecture, Rebecca on data infrastructure and technical legibility, Johny on research synthesis layer.
Building a research registry that can be trusted at institutional grade
Strong fit
Why strong
Johny's 50:1 research-to-output methodology and Rebecca's source-first epistemology are exactly what an auditable, versioned research primitive layer requires. This is not a market most teams can serve because they don't hold the standard long enough.
Risk
Depth can outpace product. The registry can become a research project instead of a product. Needs hard scope limits per vertical.
Who leads
Johny on research architecture and mapping, Rebecca on data infrastructure and schema.
Decision coaching — the framework underneath the process step
Strong fit
Why strong
The team doesn't just think in tasks — they think in the reasoning underneath tasks. Kelly explicitly models the emotional and relational layer under every structural decision. Johny maps the knowledge terrain. Rebecca stress-tests against source. That's a coaching loop.
Risk
The coaching output can become too comprehensive. Customers want the 3-sentence insight, not the 12-page framework behind it. Compression is the skill to develop explicitly.
Who leads
Kelly on relational and decision architecture, Johny on content synthesis, Rebecca on structural output quality.
Building the composition engine — applying research to specific company contexts
Mixed
Why mixed
The research and architecture are strong fits. The customization-at-scale problem — applying primitives to thousands of different company contexts without losing quality — is an engineering and product problem that requires velocity the team will resist.
Risk
The team will want to perfect the composition logic before shipping it. The market wants to see it work on real companies. Tension between quality standard and iteration speed.
Who leads
Rebecca on technical architecture, but the team needs an external forcing function to ship iterations faster than feels comfortable.
02 — Go-to-market

Customer discovery & market positioning

Identifying who the first customers actually are
Good fit
Why good
Kelly's instinct + research + validate cycle is well-suited to customer discovery when the question is "what do they actually need, not what do they say they need." Johny maps the landscape of possible customers fast. The team can do rigorous discovery.
Risk
Will over-research the customer definition instead of talking to 10 people in a week. "CEOs and VCs who can move quickly" is a placeholder — someone needs to name specific people and call them this week.
Who leads
Kelly on relational outreach, Johny on landscape mapping of customer types, Rebecca on technical buyer criteria.
Compressing the full thesis into a 2-minute external narrative
Caution
Why caution
All three naturally produce more comprehensive output than the audience needs. Johny's "everything connects" thesis, Kelly's system architectures, Rebecca's structural precision — none of these compress naturally to the entry point a first-time buyer needs. This is the team's sharpest communication gap.
Risk
The pitch will be true, defensible, and too complex for a 20-minute intro meeting. The team needs to build the 2-minute version first and work backward — not forward from the full thesis.
Who leads
Kelly is best positioned to hold the external-facing frame, but she needs a specific audience brief to compress toward. Nobody on the team naturally simplifies without a forcing function.
Competitive positioning — what makes COGS different from existing tools
Mixed
Why mixed
The team can produce the most rigorous competitive analysis in the room. What's harder is compressing it to the 3-sentence version that lands in a sales conversation without losing the substance. Johny mapped the AI company landscape in the 2/26 session with precision — but needs the brief to compress it for a specific buyer.
Risk
The competitive POV gets built for internal clarity and never adapted for customer use. "We're not a task management tool" is clear internally; it's not a positioning statement externally.
Who leads
Johny on research and mapping, Kelly on framing for external audiences, Rebecca on technical differentiation claims.
High-volume outbound sales — moving fast across many prospects
Weak fit
Why weak
The team's process is built for depth, not volume. High-velocity outbound requires a playbook the team hasn't built, a tolerance for 60% messaging, and iteration speed that runs against the collective instinct. Not impossible — just the wrong mode for how these three think.
Risk
If outbound is required early, the team will build a beautiful outbound framework and send 5 emails. They won't send 500.
Who leads
This is a hire or a constraint — not a founder-led motion. Route early customers through warm networks instead (Fidelity, Zachary Burns, Geraldine). Outbound can come after product-market fit proof.
03 — Domain expertise

Impact investing & philanthropic infrastructure

Mapping the philanthropic capital landscape — who gives what, to whom, and why
Strong fit
Why strong
Rebecca's 990 parsing infrastructure and foundation research work, Johny's 350+ org mapping methodology, and Kelly's fiscal sponsorship pressure-testing (Realize Impact vs DAF analysis) represent genuine research depth in this landscape. The team has already done work most analysts haven't.
Risk
The landscape mapping can become a product in itself rather than informing the product. The IRS 990 data infrastructure was a deep dive — clarify what it's in service of.
Who leads
Rebecca on data infrastructure and parsing, Johny on synthesis across organizations, Kelly on structural and governance analysis.
Governance innovation — steward-ownership, dual structures, non-extractive models
Strong fit
Why strong
The team has thought harder about entity structure, governance philosophy, and alternative capital models than most founding teams — because they're building one. Johny's cross-domain synthesis (Indigenous governance + corporate structures), Kelly's "what is the architecture that produces the right outcome" instinct, and Rebecca's research depth on steward-ownership give the team genuine expertise.
Risk
The intellectual work is stronger than the product work in this area right now. Needs to move from analysis to implementation.
Who leads
Johny on cross-domain synthesis, Kelly on structural architecture, Rebecca on technical implementation of dual structures.
Bridging impact investors with regenerative solutions — the matching problem
Good fit
Why good
Rebecca's Ethic and Capricorn backgrounds, Johny's synthesis across impact investing asset classes, and the team's deep knowledge of how capital actually flows in this ecosystem are genuine advantages. The matching problem requires understanding both sides with depth — rare for any single team to have.
Risk
The team understands the investor side better than the solution side at this point. The regenerative solutions landscape requires as much mapping work as the investor landscape did.
Who leads
Rebecca on investor-side infrastructure (from Ethic/Capricorn experience), Johny on regenerative solutions landscape synthesis.
Conservation biology data infrastructure — Amazon biodiversity, nature finance
Mixed
Why mixed
Rebecca's deep interest in conservation biology and Amazon mapping is genuine, and Johny's cross-domain capacity could synthesize this with nature finance structures. The domain is real. The question is whether it's core product or adjacent research — and that's not resolved.
Risk
Intellectually compelling rabbit hole that is genuinely not the same problem as COGS. The team's "everything connects" bias can make this feel like core work when it's actually valuable but peripheral.
Who leads
Rebecca on data infrastructure, but this needs a deliberate scoping decision about its relationship to core product before it consumes bandwidth.
04 — Technical problems

Data infrastructure & system architecture

Designing the technical architecture for COGS — what the system actually is
Strong fit
Why strong
Rebecca's technical depth in data infrastructure and PostgreSQL/vector database work, combined with Kelly's system architecture instinct and Johny's research layer conceptualization, produces a genuinely strong technical design team. The 2/26 session architecture (research registry, composition engine, process framework) reflects this.
Risk
Architecture can become more complete than necessary before shipping. The system is comprehensive — needs a "what is v1?" constraint applied hard.
Who leads
Rebecca on technical execution, Kelly on system architecture and governance, Johny on research layer design.
Context management and LLM integration patterns
Good fit
Why good
Rebecca's LLM integration work and vector database experience are directly relevant. The team has a genuine conceptual framework for what context management means organizationally (from 2/26), which is more than most teams building in this space.
Risk
The technical problem (context compression, cost-performance trade-offs) is still an open research question the team named in 2/26. Needs more clarity before architecture decisions are finalized.
Who leads
Rebecca on technical implementation and API patterns.
Shipping a working demo fast enough to generate early signal
Caution
Why caution
The "in-meeting experience" idea from 2/26 is the right instinct — demonstrate the product's value by pre-seeding context in a live meeting. Getting to a demo that works on real data in front of a real customer requires the team to ship before it's perfect. That is the hardest thing for this team to do.
Risk
The demo concept expands into a full product before it gets in front of a customer. "Validate the architecture" (2/26 action item) becomes months of work instead of two weeks of scoped building.
Who leads
Rebecca on technical build, but needs an external deadline (a customer meeting with a specific date) to force the ship decision.
Cross-IDE AI configuration and developer tooling
Mixed
Why mixed
Rebecca has done the work here and has strong opinions. It's a real technical problem with a practical solution. The question is whether it's a product or a side project — and whether it generalizes beyond internal use.
Risk
Interesting technical work that doesn't connect to COGS and consumes Rebecca's bandwidth in a way that's hard to justify if COGS is the core product.
Who leads
Rebecca. But needs a deliberate decision about whether this is core, adjacent, or paused.
05 — Internal operations

Team operations & organizational design

Designing the entity and governance structure for New Radicle itself
Good fit
Why good
Kelly's entity decomposition work (LLC vs C Corp, governance relationships, phased independence) is exactly the kind of structural design the team is good at. The fact that they are the customer for this problem means the feedback loop is tight — they'll know immediately if the architecture doesn't work.
Risk
The "founders' alignment package" Kelly mentioned — four deliverables for a conversation between three people who already trust each other — is a real risk. They can over-design their own governance before they've generated revenue.
Who leads
Kelly on structural architecture, Rebecca on legal/technical infrastructure, Johny on governance philosophy research.
Building decision-making norms and operating rhythms for the founding team
Caution
Why caution
The team understands this problem better than almost any founding team — they literally wrote working guides for each other. The caution is that designing the operating system and running it are different skills. The norms exist. The question is whether they're being practiced or just documented.
Risk
Three people who all think carefully about process design can produce beautiful operating frameworks that don't change behavior. The 70% vs 95% decision norm, the phase-naming protocol, the compression brief requirement — these need to be habits, not documents.
Who leads
Kelly on relational and process architecture, but all three have to hold each other to the norms. Nobody gets to opt out of calling the 70% decision.
Hiring and scaling the team from 3 to 15 people
Weak fit
Why weak
The 2/26 notes explicitly said "it would be very difficult to bring someone else in quickly given the depth of thinking and unique working model." That's an honest self-assessment — and it's also a scaling problem. The team is cognitively dense. Onboarding new people who think differently will be friction-heavy.
Risk
The team hires for cognitive similarity (also thorough, also high-standard) and ends up with no velocity drivers. Or hires fast operators who can't integrate with the existing culture and churn.
Who leads
Kelly on cultural and relational fit criteria. The first hire should be the velocity driver the team is missing — someone who ships before it's perfect and has strong enough signal to know when 70% is enough.
Managing timelines and milestones under self-imposed deadlines
Mixed
Why mixed
All three are capable of disciplined execution when external deadlines exist (investor meeting, customer demo, funding close). The challenge is self-imposed timelines — none of the three are natural "I will ship this by Friday regardless" operators. The team's milestone culture needs external pressure to function well.
Risk
"Define timeline and milestones for next week" was a 2/26 action item. Whether that happened is the leading indicator for whether this is actually a problem.
Who leads
Whoever has the most external accountability in a given week. The team should explicitly track which founder is holding the deadline for each workstream.
