00 — Who Each Person Is

The one-line version of each founder

Before the comparisons: a grounded summary of each person's actual cognitive signature, drawn from their own docs.

Rebecca

Source-first, bottom-up architect

Goes to the raw material before forming positions. Sequential, deep processor. Builds mental models from foundational elements rather than accepting existing frameworks. Perfects the vessel — precision and architecture before features. Once convinced, ships confidently.

Johny

Wide-range synthesizer and compressor

Runs 5–8 parallel workstreams simultaneously. Maps exhaustive terrain, then compresses hard — 50:1 research-to-output ratios. Sees everything as connected. Needs constraints to compress efficiently. Cross-domain leap is where he finds the value.

Kelly

Systems thinker who leads with instinct

Knows what she thinks before she can say why. Doesn't commit until she's built the scaffolding — research, comparable models, framework. Thinks in architectures, not tasks. Finds the relational layer under every structural question. Once she commits, she doesn't relitigate.

01 — Pair Dynamics

How each pair fits together

Three pairs worth understanding independently: Rebecca × Johny (the technical and strategic core), Kelly × Johny (the synthesis and systems pair), and Rebecca × Kelly (the precision and architecture pair).

Rebecca × Johny

The clearest division of labor on the team

Johny maps the landscape and compresses; Rebecca verifies the synthesis against source material and stress-tests it. He needs constraints to compress faster — she's the person who provides them ("the audience is a 60-year-old family office CIO"). She needs source material, not summaries — he produces it at scale. Primary friction: both hold things open looking for blind spots. Velocity vs. completeness is the named risk. Requires an explicit norm around 70% vs 95% decisions.

Kelly × Johny

Similar instincts, very different processing styles

Both see patterns across domains. Both decompose before deciding. Both think in systems. But Kelly processes sequentially through her 6-phase cycle (Orient → Sense-check → Research → Synthesize → Pressure-test → Commit), while Johny runs multiple parallel expansions and compressions. Kelly commits cleanly at the end of her process; Johny loops back through the second expansion. Risk: Johny still exploring when Kelly is ready to execute. Strength: Kelly's "what's actually being decided here?" orientation can snap Johny's expansive framing into the right question fast.

Rebecca × Kelly

The precision pair — strongest on architecture and legibility

Both demand structural integrity. Kelly decomposes the decision architecture before answering; Rebecca builds mental models from foundational elements before forming positions. Both are perfectionists about the vessel. Both push back on confident answers that don't pass their smell test. Risk: two people who both want to get it right before moving — without Johny or an external deadline, they can extend the research phase past its useful point. Strength: when they agree, the output is genuinely institutional-grade.

02 — What This Team Does Well

Team strengths

These are structural advantages that emerge from the specific combination of all three. Not individual strengths — things the team can do that none of them could do as well alone.

  • Full-stack due diligence
    Johny maps the landscape exhaustively. Rebecca verifies the synthesis against source. Kelly decomposes the decision architecture underneath. Together, this is a genuinely rigorous process — the kind that produces work that holds up under the scrutiny of the most sophisticated investors and partners. This is a real competitive advantage for COGS positioning.
  • Self-correcting on framing
    Kelly's first instinct is "what are we actually deciding?" Johny's is "what's missing?" Rebecca's is "have we looked at the source?" Any one of these alone can miss problems. All three together means it's very hard for the team to get stuck answering the wrong question for long.
  • Architecture → execution loop
    Kelly builds the system architecture; Rebecca verifies it structurally from the ground up; Johny compresses it into a form that can be communicated externally. The loop from "what are we actually building?" to "how do we tell this story?" has all three stages covered.
  • Prototype to think
    All three share some version of "build to clarify, not plan then build." This means the team won't get stuck in extended pre-planning phases. They'll move to tangible artifacts quickly, which is how the best early-stage companies operate.
  • Stress-testing as culture
    Johny looks for what's missing. Kelly pressure-tests against the strongest counter-argument she can find. Rebecca builds conviction by going to source material. This team will surface the hard objections internally before anyone else does — which is enormously valuable in a capital-raising environment.
  • Instinct + validation loop
    Kelly leads with instinct but doesn't trust it until validated. Johny's cross-domain leaps generate hypotheses fast. Rebecca's source-first process validates or challenges them. This is a generative loop: fast conjecture, rigorous testing, clean output.
03 — Where The Team Is Exposed

Team blind spots

These are structural vulnerabilities — patterns that show up because of how all three people are wired, not in spite of it.

The core risks
Over-researching medium-stakes decisions All three have high research instincts and perfectionist standards. Kelly says it directly: her research phase "is most likely to need a deadline imposed on it." Rebecca's process requires deep intake before convergence. Johny's expansions loop. The team will tend to apply 95% process to 70% decisions. Nobody has a natural shortcut instinct.
Mistaking process for progress Kelly named this about herself: "designing how to make a decision can feel like making the decision." The founders' alignment package she references — four deliverables for a conversation between three people who already trust each other — is a team-level version of this. Worth watching at the group level.
No natural velocity driver None of the three founders is wired as a "ship it now, fix it later" operator. Rebecca needs conviction first. Johny loops back. Kelly has a long ramp-up. This is fine for quality. It means the team will need external pressure (investor timelines, customer commitments, deadlines) to maintain shipping tempo at the right stages.
The subtler risks
Under-communicating phase status Kelly explicitly says she can under-communicate how far along she is. Johny's cycles aren't always legible to others. Rebecca has extended deep-intake phases that look like silence. Three people who all process privately and signal convergence inconsistently — the team needs an explicit "where are you on this?" norm, not as a courtesy but as an operating protocol.
No natural external translator Johny asks Rebecca and Kelly to hold the "which connections to make explicit for which audience?" frame. Kelly thinks in systems that come out more comprehensive than requested. Rebecca builds architecturally. The team is extremely good at internal rigor and not naturally wired for simplification-for-external-audiences. Customer discovery and sales positioning will require explicit effort.
Everything connects to everything Johny's thesis, Kelly's systems orientation, Rebecca's bottom-up integration — all three naturally see more connections than a customer or investor needs to see. The team can build a framework so complete it becomes hard to enter. Someone needs to hold the "what is the one thing?" question in customer-facing moments.

The honest read

Right now, all three are in their peak phases at the same time.

0→1 thesis formation and 1→10 early product are exactly where this team's cognitive profiles are most load-bearing. Johny's synthesis capacity, Rebecca's technical and architectural precision, and Kelly's instinct-driven systems thinking are all maximally useful right now. The challenge isn't capability — it's coordination velocity. The team needs to build explicit protocols for the 10→50 phase before it arrives, because that's the phase where none of the three is naturally wired for the primary demands.

05 — Operating Protocols

What to actually do about this

Naming the patterns is only useful if it generates specific behavior changes. These are the concrete norms worth building into how the team operates — not aspirational, just mechanical.

  • Name your phase out loud
    Johny is mid-expansion or compressing. Kelly is exploring or converging. Rebecca is in deep intake or ready to decide. These are different states that require different responses from co-founders. The team should name them explicitly in real time — "I'm still in intake on this" or "I'm at convergence, I need real dialogue" — rather than assuming the other person can read it. Kelly says she's working on this. Make it a team norm.
  • 70% vs 95% flag
    Before going into research mode on any decision, name the stakes: is this a 70% decision (needs to be good enough, time matters more) or a 95% decision (needs to be right, quality matters more)? The team has no natural shortcut instinct — so the shortcut has to be procedural. Kelly explicitly says "tell her when the team has researched enough." She'll trust the read. Build a norm where any founder can call this.
  • Give Johny constraints early
    Johny's compression quality is directly tied to the specificity of the brief. "What's the audience?" "What decision does this output need to enable?" "What's the format?" Providing these upfront — rather than waiting for a draft and reacting — saves a full expansion/compression cycle and produces better output.
  • Kelly holds the customer frame
    When Johny and Rebecca are both inside a thesis (which will happen often), Kelly's structural role is to hold the external-facing question: "Would a customer understand this?" "What does this actually solve for them?" This isn't a constraint on the thinking — it's the translation layer the team is structurally missing without it.
  • Rebecca goes to source, others give her the source
    Rebecca will find the original data anyway. Giving her source material rather than summaries saves time and produces a cleaner position faster. When Johny does research, packaging it in a navigable form (not a 25,000-word brief without structure) is worth the extra 20 minutes.
  • Start building before you're ready
    All three share the "prototype to think" instinct — but all three also have perfectionist tendencies that can delay the first tangible artifact. The team should use this shared belief as a forcing function: when a conversation is going in circles, build something small and ugly and use it to move the thinking forward. The artifact will do what the conversation can't.
The closing note

The team's thesis and its execution risk are the same thing.

All three founders believe that the quality of the thinking — the decomposition, the architecture, the precision — is load-bearing. They're right. That rigor is what will make COGS credible to the most sophisticated audiences. The risk is that the same instinct that produces extraordinary work also produces the conditions for missed windows. The team knows this about themselves. The question is whether they build explicit norms around it, or rely on good intentions. Good intentions haven't worked for any founding team at speed. The norms will.
