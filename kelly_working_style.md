# Kelly — Working Style

New Radicle · Co-Founder Reference · March 2026

---

## The one-line version

Systems thinker who leads with instinct. Knows what she thinks before she can say why. Does not commit until she has built the scaffolding: the research, comparable models, the framework. Thinks in architectures, not tasks. Once she commits, she does not relitigate.

---

## How she processes

**Pattern 01 — She decomposes before she decides**
When Kelly encounters a big question, her first instinct is never to answer it. It is to break it apart. She needs to understand the sub-components, the dependencies, the adjacent questions that might change the answer. She is often the person saying "wait, before we decide that, we need to figure out X" — which can feel like stalling when she is actually making sure the team solves the right problem. This is probably her strongest cognitive habit. It shows up in everything: entity structure, product decisions, even personal purchases.

**Pattern 02 — She thinks in systems, not tasks**
Her brain does not naturally produce to-do lists. It produces architectures. Hand her a problem and she will hand back a structure. She is usually designing how to approach something rather than diving straight into doing it. When the team needed to align on product roadmaps, her instinct was not "let us merge the three docs" — it was "how should we structure the process for merging them so the output is something everyone owns." She turned a synthesis task into an organizational design question.

**Pattern 03 — She validates instincts with research, then loops back**
She almost always has a strong initial instinct. But she does not trust it until she has pressure-tested it. Her process: gut reaction, research, then either confirm and strengthen, or discover she was wrong and adjust. She rarely skips the research step even when fairly confident, because she has been surprised enough times to know her instincts have blind spots. The tell is whether she has asked for research or comparison yet. If she has, the decision is being actively processed.

**Pattern 04 — She iterates openly and upgrades without embarrassment**
She does not hold onto previous positions because she said them out loud. If she sent something last week and has since found a better approach, she will come back and say so. She does not experience this as failure — she experiences it as the process working. Early outputs are v1s. If she means "this is locked," she will say so explicitly.

**Pattern 05 — She finds the relational layer underneath the structural one**
Behind every structural or strategic question, Kelly sees a human question. "What is the right equity split?" becomes "what does each person need this to be worth, and what does the split say about how we see each other?" She believes the personal layer is the actual foundation of every business decision. If it is skipped, the structural stuff eventually breaks. This is the pattern most likely to either resonate deeply or feel like over-processing depending on the moment.

**Pattern 06 — She pushes back on confident answers that do not pass her smell test**
She does not defer to expertise or authority when something feels off. If someone gives her a confident-sounding answer and it does not match what she thinks she knows, she will challenge it — not aggressively, but persistently. This means she will sometimes push back on things the other person is more expert in than she is. That is not distrust. It is trying to understand something well enough to trust it.

## Her problem-solving sequence

1. **Orient** — What are we actually deciding? Decompose into sub-parts, dependencies, adjacent questions that might change the answer.
2. **Sense-check** — What does my gut say? Register the instinct as a hypothesis to test, not a conclusion to defend.
3. **Research** — What do comparable cases, data, and frameworks say? This is the step most likely to need a deadline imposed on it.
4. **Synthesize** — Build a framework or structure that organizes what she has learned. Output is often more comprehensive than what was asked for.
5. **Pressure-test** — What is she missing? Ask for critical feedback. Test against the strongest counter-argument she can find.
6. **Commit** — Once she has landed, she commits. Does not relitigate unless genuinely new information appears.

---

## What helps her do her best work

- Give her context before asking for a decision — even a sentence of "here is why this matters now" helps her orient faster.
- Push back on her frameworks. If she is overbuilding something, the most useful thing to say is "I think we just need to decide X."
- Share your early, rough thinking. She is much better at improving something that exists than starting from nothing together.
- Tell her when she is going too deep. "This is a 70% decision, not a 95% decision" — she will be grateful.
- Give her a deadline when she is in research mode. She will hear it as helpful, not pressuring.

## What creates friction

- Asking her to commit to something she has not had time to think through. She will either give a weak answer or push back on the timeline.
- Treating early outputs as final positions. If she shares something, it is probably a v1.
- Skipping the "why" and jumping to execution. She needs to understand the reasoning, not just the plan. This is how she builds conviction.
- Presenting a fait accompli and asking her to ratify it. She wants to be in the decision, not approve it after the fact.

## Translation layer

The space between what Kelly does and what it can look like from the outside. The same behavior lands very differently depending on whether you understand the intent behind it.

| What it looks like | What is actually happening |
|--------------------|----------------------------|
| She is stalling on this decision | She is decomposing it into sub-questions because the framing feels wrong. Ask: "What question do you think we should actually be answering?" |
| She is overthinking this | She is in research mode, building conviction. The decision is being actively processed. Give her a deadline. |
| She keeps changing her mind | She is iterating. Early outputs are v1s, and she upgrades when she finds better information. Ask: "Is this still in draft, or are you converging?" |
| She is making this too personal | She is looking for the relational layer underneath the structural decision. If the relational lens is not adding value, say so directly. She will calibrate. |
| She is being difficult about my proposal | She is stress-testing it. The pushback is how she processes agreement. Let her push. If she stops asking questions, she is either convinced or disengaged — you want the former. |

## Watch-outs

**She can apply 95% process to 70% decisions.** The research phase is most likely to need a deadline imposed on it. When time matters more than precision, tell her. She will trust the team's read on when enough research is enough.

**She can mistake process design for progress.** Designing how to make a decision can feel like making the decision. She has noticed this pattern in herself and adjusted, but it is worth watching at the team level — especially when all three founders are in a session together.

**She can under-communicate how far along she is.** Because she iterates and upgrades, it is not always clear whether she is in exploring mode or converging mode. Ask her directly. She will tell you honestly.

**She sometimes makes things more personal than they need to be.** The relational-layer pattern serves the team well for the big decisions. But not every question has a hidden emotional dimension. Sometimes the org chart is just an org chart.
