# Rebecca — Working Style

*New Radicle · Co-Founder Reference · March 2026*

---

## The one-line version

Source-first, bottom-up architect. Goes to the raw material before forming positions. Builds mental models from foundational elements rather than accepting existing frameworks. Once convinced, ships confidently.

---

## How she processes

**Pattern 01 — Source material first**
Rebecca goes to the original data before building any model. She will find the primary source anyway — so give it to her upfront rather than a summary. This isn't stubbornness; it's how she builds conviction that holds under pressure. Pre-digested frameworks get interrogated, not accepted.

**Pattern 02 — Sequential, deep processing**
She doesn't run parallel workstreams. She goes deep on one thing at a time, in protected focus, and surfaces with a formed view. Interrupting this process mid-cycle produces worse output than letting it complete. The silence is the process.

**Pattern 03 — Bottom-up model building**
She builds mental models from foundational elements up. She doesn't accept the existing framework and populate it — she decomposes the problem first, then reconstructs. The reconstruction is more defensible but takes longer than top-down approaches.

**Pattern 04 — Precision in language**
Naming is load-bearing for her. The wrong name misdirects everything built on it. She notices when something is said imprecisely and will push on it — not as a stylistic preference but as an architectural concern. If she's asking you to say it differently, she thinks the current framing will cause a problem later.

**Pattern 05 — Build to think**
The artifact is the thinking, not the output of thinking. She prototypes to clarify, not after clarity is reached. If a conversation is going in circles, she'll want to build something concrete and use it to move the discussion forward.

**Pattern 06 — Perfectionist standard**
"Institutional grade or nothing" — the standard is always whether this would hold up under scrutiny from the most sophisticated person in the room. This is a genuine strength and a source of velocity risk on medium-stakes decisions.

---

## Her problem-solving sequence

1. **Find the source** — What is the actual primary data here? Not the summary, not the interpretation.
2. **Decompose** — Break the problem into its foundational components before building any model.
3. **Build the model** — Construct upward from the elements. This takes time and resists shortcuts.
4. **Form a position** — The position emerges from the model, not before it.
5. **Pressure-test** — Does this hold? What am I missing?
6. **Ship** — Once convinced, she moves confidently and doesn't relitigate.

---

## What helps her do her best work

- Give her source material, not summaries. She'll find the original anyway.
- Let her complete the intake phase before asking for a position. She'll give a weak answer if pushed before she's ready.
- Be precise in language — vague framing is friction for her.
- Build something tangible when the conversation stalls. She'll engage better with a concrete artifact than with continued discussion.
- Tell her which decisions are 70% calls vs. 95% calls. She'll apply the right standard if she knows which is which.

## What creates friction

- Being asked for a position before she's built the model.
- Receiving summaries or secondhand analysis when the source is available.
- Imprecise language that names things incorrectly at the foundational level.
- Open-ended scope without defined audience or constraint — she needs the brief to build toward something.
- Extended uncertainty after she's reached convergence. Once she's at Phase 5, she wants to close.

---

## Working with Rebecca on decisions

Her default is construction: she builds the model and the position emerges from it. She's not looking for the gap that invalidates (that's Johny's mode) — she's completing the architecture. These two modes can talk past each other in live decision moments. Neither is wrong. The key is knowing which mode each person is in.

When she pushes back on a proposed direction, she's usually not rejecting it — she's checking whether the framing is right before building on it. Ask what specifically feels off. The answer will be architectural.

---

## Collaboration signals

| Signal | What it means | How to respond |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| She goes quiet for a few days | She's in deep intake mode, building the model | Don't interrupt. A simple "where are you on X?" is fine. |
| She asks for the source document | She's not satisfied with the summary | Send the original, not a better summary |
| She pushes back on the name/framing | She thinks the current framing is structurally wrong | Engage with the specific concern, don't defend the name |
| She says "I think I'm ready" | She's at convergence, wants real dialogue | Have the actual decision conversation now |
| She ships something | She's fully convinced — don't relitigate | Execute with her |

---

## Watch-outs

**She can extend the research phase past its useful point on medium-stakes decisions.** The process that produces extraordinary work on high-stakes decisions gets applied to everything. Someone else on the team needs to name when enough research is enough.

**Her convergence can look like silence before it becomes clarity.** Build in check-ins if you need to track her phase.

**She needs a defined audience or constraint to compress toward.** The constraint isn't limiting — it's what makes the output sharper.
